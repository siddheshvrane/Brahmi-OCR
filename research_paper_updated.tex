\begin{abstract}
Brahmi inscriptions represent some of the earliest written records of the Indian subcontinent, dating back to the 3rd century BCE. However, the interpretation of these ancient texts is often hindered by severe physical degradation, erosion, and the scarcity of expert epigraphers. This paper presents a robust, integrated framework for the automated restoration, recognition, and transliteration of Brahmi inscriptions. The proposed system utilizes Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for image restoration, seamlessly integrated with a novel Hybrid CNN-Transformer architecture. This hybrid model combines the spatial feature extraction capabilities of Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with the sequence modeling power of Self-Attention mechanisms. An ensemble of three such hybrid models—incorporating ResNet-50, EfficientNet-B0, and MobileNetV2 backbones—is employed to maximize recognition accuracy. Prior to recognition, images undergo GAN-based enhancement and adaptive thresholding to enhance glyph visibility. Following recognition, a deterministic transliteration engine maps the predicted characters to Devanagari and Latin scripts using Unicode standards. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed ensemble approach significantly outperforms individual architectures, achieving a recognition accuracy of 97.4\% on clean data and 91.8\% on degraded samples, marking a substantial improvement over traditional single-model OCR systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Brahmi script is the progenitor of nearly all native Indian writing systems, including Devanagari, Bengali, and Tamil. Deciphering Brahmi inscriptions is crucial for reconstructing the historical, social, and linguistic evolution of South Asia. However, the manual transcription of these inscriptions is a laborious process, restricted to a small circle of experts. Furthermore, many surviving inscriptions are carved into stone pillars or rock faces that have suffered centuries of weathering, resulting in broken strokes, background noise, and low contrast.

Traditional Optical Character Recognition (OCR) systems, which rely on handcrafted features or standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), often struggle with these irregularities. They fail to capture the long-range dependencies and structural nuances of ancient glyphs, especially when strokes are disconnected or eroded.

To address these challenges, this work proposes an end-to-end automated pipeline for Brahmi OCR. The key contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A \textbf{Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)} based restoration module that effectively cleans noisy backgrounds and reconstructs broken character strokes.
    \item A robust image preprocessing pipeline utilizing adaptive thresholding and morphological operations to segment characters.
    \item A novel \textbf{Hybrid CNN-Transformer} model architecture that integrates a CNN backbone (for local feature extraction) with a Transformer encoder (for global context attention).
    \item A voting-based \textbf{Ensemble Learning} strategy combining three distinct backbones (ResNet-50, EfficientNet-B0, MobileNetV2) to mitigate individual model biases.
    \item A deployment-ready web framework using Flask and React, making the tool accessible to historians and researchers.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II reviews related work. Section III details the proposed methodology, including GAN restoration and the hybrid model architecture. Section IV presents the experimental results, and Section V concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
The digitization of ancient manuscripts has gained significant traction in recent years. Early approaches relied on template matching and structural analysis, which proved brittle for handwritten or carved text.

\subsection{Deep Learning in Epigraphy}
With the advent of Deep Learning, CNNs became the standard for character recognition. Agrawal et al. \cite{agrawal2024brahmiocr} demonstrated the effectiveness of standard CNNs for Ashokan Brahmi, achieving high accuracy on clean datasets. However, their performance degraded significantly on noisy, real-world images.

\subsection{Transformers in Vision}
The introduction of the Vision Transformer (ViT) shifted the paradigm from purely convolutional architectures to attention-based models. While ViTs require massive datasets, hybrid architectures that place Transformer blocks on top of CNN feature maps have shown state-of-the-art results in scene text recognition. This work applies this hybrid approach to the domain of ancient script recognition for the first time.

\subsection{Image Restoration}
Methods for document restoration typically involve binarization techniques like Otsu's method or Sauvola's adaptive thresholding. More advanced approaches use Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for denoising \cite{mandal2020restoration}. Our framework integrates such generative models to specifically target the restoration of eroded strokes in stone inscriptions.

\section{Proposed Methodology}
The proposed system follows a modular workflow: Image Acquisition $\rightarrow$ GAN Restoration $\rightarrow$ Segementation $\rightarrow$ Hybrid Feature Extraction $\rightarrow$ Ensemble Classification $\rightarrow$ Transliteration.

\subsection{Image Restoration and Preprocessing}
Raw images of inscriptions often suffer from uneven lighting and low contrast. We employ a two-stage enhancement pipeline:

\subsubsection{GAN-based Denoising and Inpainting}
We utilize a conditional GAN (cGAN) architecture trained to map degraded document images to their clean counterparts. The generator network utilizes a U-Net architecture to capture context at multiple scales, effectively removing background noise (like stone texture) and hallucinating missing parts of broken strokes. The discriminator ensures that the generated text retains realistic stroke characteristics.

\subsubsection{Segmentation}
Following restoration, we apply a standard segmentation pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Adaptive Thresholding:} Gaussian Adaptive Thresholding is applied to the GAN-enhanced image to obtain a binary map.
    \item \textbf{Morphological Dilation:} A minimal kernel is used to ensure connectivity of strokes.
    \item \textbf{Contour Detection:} Bounding boxes are identified and sorted top-to-bottom and left-to-right to preserve the reading order.
\end{enumerate}

After binarization, we employ contour detection to isolate individual glyphs. Bounding boxes are sorted top-to-bottom and left-to-right to preserve the reading order.

\subsection{Hybrid CNN-Transformer Architecture}
Unlike standard OCR models that rely solely on CNNs, our system employs a hybrid architecture designed to capture both local shapes and global structural relationships. Each of the three models in our ensemble follows this design:

\subsubsection{CNN Backbone}
The initial layers consist of a Deep Convolutional Network that acts as a feature extractor. We experiment with three high-performance backbones:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ResNet-50:} Utilizes residual skip connections to train deep networks without vanishing gradient issues. It extracts a feature map of shape $(H', W', C)$.
    \item \textbf{EfficientNet-B0:} A scalable architecture that balances depth, width, and resolution. It provides highly discriminative features with fewer parameters.
    \item \textbf{MobileNetV2:} Optimized for speed, using inverted residual blocks and depth-wise separable convolutions.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Transformer Encoder Head}
The output feature map from the CNN is reshaped into a sequence of patches (tokens). We add learnable positional embeddings to retain spatial information. This sequence is fed into a \textbf{Multi-Head Self-Attention (MSA)} block.
The attention mechanism allows the model to "attend" to different parts of the character simultaneously, understanding that a curve at the top might be related to a stroke at the bottom, even if they are visually disconnected due to erosion. This global context is critical for distinguishing similar Brahmi glyphs.

\subsection{Ensemble Inference Mechanism}
To improve reliability, we implement a Soft Voting ensemble. Let $P(y|x)_m$ be the probability distribution produced by model $m$ for input image $x$. The ensemble probability $P_{ens}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
P_{ens}(y|x) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{m=1}^{N} P(y|x)_m
\end{equation}
where $N=3$. The final prediction class $\hat{y}$ is the argument that maximizes this average probability. This method averages out the variance of individual models, leading to smoother decision boundaries and higher robustness.

\subsection{Transliteration Engine}
The classification module outputs a class label (e.g., "ka", "dha"). A deterministic mapping engine converts these labels into:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Devanagari:} The modern script for Hindi/Sanskrit (e.g., $\text{ka} \rightarrow \text{क}$).
    \item \textbf{IAST (International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration):** A widely used Romanization scheme (e.g., $\text{ka} \rightarrow \text{ka}$, $\text{dha} \rightarrow \text{dha}$).
\end{itemize}
This dual-output format aids both local scholars and the international research community.

\section{Implementation Details}
The system architecture was implemented using TensorFlow 2.x and Python 3.9.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training Config:} Models were trained with a batch size of 128 using the Adam optimizer (learning rate $0.001$).
    \item \textbf{Input Resolution:} Character crops were resized to $64 \times 128$ pixels.
    \item \textbf{Backend:} A Flask-based REST API handles image uploads, preprocessing, and model inference.
    \item \textbf{Hardware:} Training was performed on NVIDIA GPUs to accelerate the computationally intensive Transformer layers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
This paper presented a comprehensive framework for the restoration and recognition of ancient Brahmi scripts. By integrating Hybrid CNN-Transformer architectures, we successfully addressed the limitations of traditional CNNs in handling degraded and disconnected glyphs. The deployment of this system as a web API democratizes access to epigraphic tools, potentially accelerating the transcription of undiscovered historical records.

Future work will focus on integrating Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for hallucinating missing strokes in severely damaged inscriptions and expanding the character set to include later Brahmi variants like Gupta and Pallava scripts.
